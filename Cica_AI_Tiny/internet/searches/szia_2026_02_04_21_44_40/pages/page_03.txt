I cannot come up with any certain information about the development of Hungarian 'szia' nor can I disprove the English (folk)-etymology; but I'd like nevertheless to give some remarks.


(I already wanted to in september when you put the question but decided against doing so, then. 
)




Firstly,
 concerning the meaning: I don't know if Hungarian 
szervusz 
is used exactly like Austrian Servus - for your information, at least in Austria 
Servus
 
both is a greeting and used for saying farewell (Wiki suggests that this were the case everywhere where it is used).

This really would indicate rather a connection of 
szia 
with 
szervusz 
than with English 'see you'.



Secondly, 
concerning the evolution of this term: it wouldn't be impossible to reconstruct a development of 
szervusz 
to 
szia
.

There exist several varieties of the greeting in Austrian dialects which have eliminated 'rv' (also mentioned at the Wiki page linked to above: 'seas') which are very common here. It is possible that either the same happened in Hungarian or that 'seas' or similar was loaned to Hungarian as 'szia'.



All still 
very 
vague, of course, and pure speculation.

Well, Zsanna, in this case "r" is in a weak position already (in Austrian German): it is vocalised to an a-shwa and thus easily dropped, so /servus/ > Austrian pronunciation /seavas/ > (with dropping of 'v') /sea'as/ > (contraction) /seas/. So the phonetic development in Austrian German isn't a mystery really.



More difficult however would be the change of /e/ to /i/ if /seas/ would be the origin for Hungarian /szia/ as Hungarian most likely would substitute this (rather open but half-long) /e/ rather with Hungarian (open, short) /e/ or at least (close, long) /é/ and not /i/. (While I don't think that dropping of /s/ poses much of a problem - this occurs frequently in words with accent on the first syllable.)



Thus my reluctancy to even post my suspicion that this (an Austrian loan) might be the case. 
 It's still possible but, as said already, all pure speculation so far.

Hi -- My parents grew up in Hungary and left in the mid 50s.  I was born there and when we moved to Canada I spoke both English and Hungarian. We never said 'szia' as a part of the Hungarian language, but we did say, 'see you' as part of the English language. When we visited Hungary in 1974, no one in Hungary said 'szia' as a greeting or a farewell, but they all said 'szerebus'.  While we were there, we said 'see ya' to our young cousins and their friends. They thought this was very amusing and imitated us and every time they saw us they would 'see ya'.  Many Canadians said 'see ya' when they went home to visit the old country.



I was shocked when I realized, very recently, that in Hungary, 'szia' as part of their daily Hungarian lexicon. 'Szia' is not a Hungarian word, nor is it German or Austrian. It is derived from the Western Canadian slang term, 'see ya' of the 1970s, brought to Hungary by tourists and visitors.  



Just ask my 85 Hungarian speaking mother, she did not grow up saying 'szia'. She'll tell you that it is a word from English, so will I. We were part of the group that started the trend.  Thanks, and see you.

I have a similar story (parents came to Canada in the 1950's), except I visited Hungary with family in the summer of 1968.  I was also shocked, just last week, to find out how common szia is now.  My sisters and I heard szia (only, not sziatok) from young kids that expected us to recognize it as an English word, this is my distinct recollection.  They were confused that I didn't immediately get it, I realized only later upon reflection.  I was 8 at the time, but the memory is strong, as indicated by how shocked I was to find out (as I say, just last week) that it is now in such common use among adults in Hungary.  The expression feels wrong to me, these were kids that got it wrong (in the sense that they said it on meeting as well as on leaving, I recall that bothering me when I figured out the english that they were copying).



For the record, the region where I heard szia for the first time was in a small town along the Austrian border, Kisnarda (or possibly the nearby town of Felsöcsatár).  I never heard it uttered by a non-english speaking Hungarian adult back in 1968.  Where did you visit in Hungary in 1974?



My parents also did not know or use szia as a Hungarian word.  My 95-year old mother said that it's an english word when I asked her just a few days ago.  I think that there should be ~70-year olds somewhere in Hungary that should also be able to corroborate the origin of szia.



Pepperheart, my experience is not to say that you didn't start a local chapter of szia enthusiasts in 1974 as you suggest.  I rather doubt the 1952 "sighting" of szia elsewhere mentioned in this thread (because of the recollections of our parents generation, and Ferenc52's comments), and I agree with you that it must have been brought to Hungary by tourists and visitors.  "See ya" is not just from Western Canada, it is common across all of North America, so lots of opportunity for the seed to be planted.  From Ferenc52's comment, it must have been in common use by the younger generation by 1979.

The origin issue for szia has been settled in this thread 
Origin or original meaning of szia
, where a 1953 document (found at 
https://real-j.mtak.hu/6027/1/MagyarNyelvor_1953.pdf
) with several instances of the word szia and an explanation of its origin is quoted.  For convenience, here is an english translation of one place in the document where origin explanation is given:



"... let us first look at 
szervusz
. It was originally the Latin 
servus
 “(your) servant”. It lived quietly for a century or so during the Latin Hungarianism of the last century, until it was Hungarianised into 
szerbusz
 and its origin almost forgotten. At the beginning of our century, under the influence of a flaunting, indulging current of francophilia showing off its language skills, it briefly sounded 
szerviő
. This 
szerviő
 didn't live long, 
szervusz
 returned, to give way to 
szevász
 about twenty years ago. Due to sloppy pronunciation, it became 
szeász
. The rest played out before our eyes and ears. 
Szeász
 transformed into 
sziász
. This became shortened to 
szia
, and because slang loves change and shortening, it became 
szi
. I wouldn't be surprised if next time I only hear a hissing sound, 
sz
, as a greeting. — 
Vilmos Zolnai"



There must have been a migration back to szia since 1953.



The word szia also appears on pg. 142 of that 1953 document. This obviously forces me to find other explanations for my experience, and for why my parents' generation (in Canada) did not use either szia or sziastok.
