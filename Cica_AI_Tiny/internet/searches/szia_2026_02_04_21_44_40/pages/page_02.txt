I know that 'szia' and its plural form 'sziasztok' are used as a friendly informal 2nd person greeting: something similar to 'hi' in English.  It would interest me to know the origin of the word: does it have any historical connection (I am thinking how the more old-fashioned alternative 'szervusz' has the notion of 'I am your servant' from the Latin for slave)?  Or could it even be a Hungarian spelling of the very informal 'see you' / 'see ya' that you hear quite often in English — British English at any rate?



Many thanks.

Agreeing with AndrasBP, I should exclude whatever English influence in this case.



From the linguistic point of view, the plural 
sziasz
tok
 
practically confirms the theory that 
szia 
comes from 
szervusz
. 
Szervusz 
comes from the Latin servus, of course, so 
szervusz
tok
 
de facto means "your servant" in plural, i.e. not "thy servant" but the servant of "you all, i.e. of more people". This 
-tok
 is not a verbal ending in this case.



(It is incredible how difficult it is to explain these things in English ..... You should have to reintroduce  words like 
thou, thee, thy, thine
 in  modern English 
 )

Some of the Hungarian diaspora (many of course left in 1956, like my mother) indicate that Szia was not present by late 1956 (of course, that needs to be qualified with "to any noticeable extent"). I would be very interested to know of any evidence of its use in the 1950's. My mother (born in 1929) says that szia is an english word. That is also my own experience, that it was presented to me as a word that I should know from English, but I didn't recognize it, at first, perhaps partly because the kids I was engaging with (on my first visit to Hungary in 1968) said it on meeting me. But "halló" also came to be used upon parting.



I do distinctly remember the disappointment of the kids who enthusiastically greeted me with szia, expecting me to be surprised and as excited as they were.  It bothered me to not get it, but it hit me later when I was alone, thinking.  I also remember feeling badly about the kids being forever confused, because I was not able to confirm that yes, I did know the word; I don't believe that I saw those kids again.



It is probably hard to find solid written evidence of early use of the word szia. But I feel that additional data could be had from the recollections of those that are now around 70 years old or so (those that were kids in the '60s), or those that are older if you believe szia started in the 1950's and/or by adults. There were almost a quarter million people that left Hungary in 1956, so more could perhaps still be surveyed for their knowledge of how they greeted each other back then. I plan to collect data from relatives to corroborate my own belief, not because I have any doubts of my own recollection (it is a strong memory), but to convince anyone else that doubts an English origin. So far, I have one sister with an experience similar to mine in 1968, and another that simply carries a belief (that is, she doesn't recall a specific experience like I had) that szia came from english. I do not have any recollection that we talked to each other or our cousins in Hungary about the word at that time, it was just a single specific encounter.

‘Alma’ and expressions of denial.
 [...] It would be sufficient to refer to the article by Béla Zolnai, which describes the simple process of the development from 
szevász
 to 
szi
 (Nyr 75 : 363, 76 : 305). Our case is identical. But for greater clarity, let us first look at 
szervusz
. It was originally the Latin 
servus
 “(your) servant”. It lived quietly for a century or so during the Latin Hungarianism of the last century, until it was Hungarianised into 
szerbusz
 and its origin almost forgotten. At the beginning of our century, under the influence of a flaunting, indulging current of francophilia showing off its language skills, it briefly sounded 
szerviő
. This 
szerviő
 didn't live long, 
szervusz
 returned, to give way to 
szevász
 about twenty years ago. Due to sloppy pronunciation, it became 
szeász
. The rest played out before our eyes and ears. 
Szeász
 transformed into 
sziász
. This became shortened to 
szia
, and because slang loves change and shortening, it became 
szi
. I wouldn't be surprised if next time I only hear a hissing sound, 
sz
, as a greeting. — 
Vilmos Zolnai

Oh my God, it is not the first time my past goes against yours.



I never used “hellóztok”, but I did use “hellótok” in Győr in my childhood.



I guess “hellósztok”  may be the result of some artificial alignment to the misinterpretated “sziasztok”. Some people must have thought that in “sziasztok” the plural was expressed by “-sztok”, because at that time “sziasz” did not exist any longer, just “szia”.



But we kids of Győr felt the boogie of etimology 
 and simply put just a “-tok” after “helló” to copy the logics of “sziasztok”.



 To be honest I heard both “hellótok” and “hellósztok” and since I do not remember I ever saw the latter in writing I cannot exclude thet “hellósztok” was not simply the pronounced “hellóztok”(NB: z is pronounced sz before the unvoiced t).  Which would be even worse than “hellósztok”.
